# Zang-Fu

## Overview

In Traditional Chinese Medicine, **Zang-Fu** is the practical synthesis where the prior frameworks meet the body. The 5 substances ([Qi.md](Qi.md), [Jing.md](Jing.md), [Shen.md](Shen.md), [Xue.md](Xue.md), [JinYe.md](JinYe.md)) and the 5 Phases ([WuXing.md](WuXing.md)) are abstract until they're attached to organ systems. Once attached, organ systems are what the meridian network ([Jingmai.md](Jingmai.md)) connects to the surface and what self-cultivation ([Qigong.md](Qigong.md)) ultimately works on.

This page is the **launch point**. It frames the Zang/Fu split, the cross-organ axes a practitioner watches first, and the diagnostic timing each organ pulses through. Per-organ depth, including patterns, personalities, and in-depth pathologies, lives on a dedicated page per organ in [Additional Reading](index.md). Twelve pages, one per channel-system organ; this page is what links into them.

Western medicine defines organs by cellular structure and physical boundaries. TCM views the Zang-Fu as functional, interconnected systems. An "organ" in TCM encompasses a physical structure, its corresponding energetic pathway (meridian), emotional state, sensory aperture, and environmental element.

## The Core Split: Zang (Yin) vs. Fu (Yang)

The internal organs are divided into two Yin-Yang paired groups based on their physiological behavior.

**Zang organs (Yin)** are solid. Their job is to manufacture, transform, and store the vital substances: Qi, Blood, Essence, Body Fluids, Spirit. They sit deep in the body and operate continuously.

**Fu organs (Yang)** are hollow. Their job is to receive, break down, transport, and eliminate food and waste. They constantly fill and empty, interacting directly with the exterior environment.

Each Zang is paired with a Fu through a shared phase. The full table:

| Phase          | Zang (Yin)                              | Fu (Yang)                                         | Pairing rationale                                                                                        |
| -------------- | --------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Wood           | [Liver (Gan)](Liver.md)                 | [Gallbladder (Dan)](Gallbladder.md)               | Liver smooths Qi flow; Gallbladder stores and discharges bile, the decision-maker.                       |
| Fire           | [Heart (Xin)](Heart.md)                 | [Small Intestine (Xiao Chang)](SmallIntestine.md) | Heart governs blood and Shen; Small Intestine sorts the pure from the turbid.                            |
| Fire (6-organ) | [Pericardium (Xin Bao)](Pericardium.md) | [San Jiao (Triple Burner)](SanJiao.md)            | The Pericardium shields the Heart; the San Jiao regulates fluid and heat across the three body cavities. |
| Earth          | [Spleen (Pi)](Spleen.md)                | [Stomach (Wei)](Stomach.md)                       | Spleen transforms and transports; Stomach receives and ripens.                                           |
| Metal          | [Lung (Fei)](Lung.md)                   | [Large Intestine (Da Chang)](LargeIntestine.md)   | Lung governs Qi and the body surface; Large Intestine completes elimination.                             |
| Water          | [Kidney (Shen)](Kidney.md)              | [Bladder (Pang Guang)](Bladder.md)                | Kidney stores Essence and governs water; Bladder removes liquid waste.                                   |

> **5 vs. 6 Zang.** The five-phase framework ([WuXing.md](WuXing.md)) uses 5 Zang (Heart, Liver, Spleen, Lung, Kidney). The meridian framework ([Jingmai.md](Jingmai.md)) uses 6 Zang by adding the **Pericardium** as a sixth Yin organ paired with the **San Jiao** as a sixth Fu, producing the 12 symmetrical channels. Both are valid; they emphasize different aspects of physiology. See the [5-vs-6 Zang reconciliation](index.md#the-5-vs-6-zang-reconciliation) for the unified view.

## The Five Shen - Organ Consciousness

Each Zang organ houses a specific aspect of consciousness (see [Shen.md](Shen.md) for the full framework):

| Organ  | Sub-spirit | Domain                                       |
| ------ | ---------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| Heart  | Shen       | Supreme consciousness, joy, clear thought    |
| Liver  | Hun        | Creativity, vision, life direction, planning |
| Spleen | Yi         | Focused thought, memory, intellectual work   |
| Lung   | Po         | Embodied awareness, grief, somatic reflexes  |
| Kidney | Zhi        | Willpower, drive, endurance, survival        |

This is a functional map, not metaphor. When a practitioner sees a patient who cannot make decisions (Hun disruption → Liver/Wood), loses their train of thought mid-sentence (Yi weakness → Spleen/Earth), or presents with fearful flatness rather than vital presence (Zhi depletion → Kidney), they are reading the Zang organ state through its resident sub-spirit. The Five Shen are the psychological readout of the Zang-Fu.

## The Heart-Kidney Axis

Heart is Fire and Kidney is Water (see [WuXing.md](WuXing.md)). In health, Kidney Yin rises to cool the Heart while Heart Fire descends to warm the Kidneys, a dialogue called _Heart-Kidney communication_ (xin shen xiang jiao). When Kidney Yin is too depleted to rise, Heart Fire runs unchecked: the classic picture is **insomnia, restless dreaming, palpitations, night sweats, and persistent low-grade anxiety**. The Shen cannot settle without Yin's anchor (see [Shen.md](Shen.md)). Treatment nourishes Kidney Yin while clearing Heart Fire. _Tian Wang Bu Xin Dan_ is canonical. Chronic overwork and late nights are typical antecedents.

## Common Combined Patterns

Organ systems rarely fail in isolation; the Five Phases cycles ([WuXing.md](WuXing.md)) propagate dysfunction across neighbors. Three combinations are especially common:

- **Liver overacting on the Spleen (Gan pi bu he).** Stagnant Liver Qi attacks adjacent Spleen, disrupting digestion and causing stress-triggered alternating bowel habits, cramping pain worsening with emotion, bloating, and irritability. The TCM pattern most often mapped to IBS; treated with _Xiao Yao San_.
- **Heart-Spleen Blood deficiency.** The Spleen builds Blood, the Heart houses Shen within it (see [Xue.md](Xue.md)). Joint depletion produces insomnia, palpitations, anxiety, poor appetite, and pale tongue as an interlocked cluster. _Gui Pi Tang_ is canonical.
- **Lung-Kidney failing to grasp Qi.** Lung sends Qi down, Kidney holds it; when Kidney Yang or Jing falters, Qi floats unanchored, causing breathlessness worse on exertion, unsatisfying inhalation, and low back weakness. Underlies chronic asthma in elderly or constitutionally weak patients.

## The Organ Clock Connection

TCM maps each organ to a 2-hour peak-Qi window in the 24-hour Organ Clock (_ziwu liuzhu_). A symptom recurring reliably at the same time of day is a pointer to the organ peaking in that window. The complete Organ Clock schedule and its diagnostic application live in [Jingmai.md](Jingmai.md). That page is the master schedule. The clinical principle here: **each organ has a rhythmic period of heightened activity**, and persistent dysfunction often reveals itself through time-specific symptom patterns rather than continuous complaints.

## Jing Conservation and the Organ Foundation

The Zang-Fu organ functions determine how much constitutional reserve (Pre-Natal Jing, described in [Jing.md](Jing.md)) the body consumes daily. **Spleen and Stomach** are the primary source of Post-Natal Jing: every meal the Spleen successfully transforms into Gu Qi, Xue, and refined essence is a deposit that offsets the daily draw on constitutional reserves. When Spleen function weakens from irregular meals, cold-raw food, or chronic worry, Post-Natal Jing fails, and the body compensates by drawing on Pre-Natal reserves. Over time, this is the mechanism behind Kidney deficiency patterns appearing in patients who are otherwise young and active.

This is the practical upshot of the Five-Phase framing in [WuXing.md](WuXing.md): Earth is the central organ pair precisely because it feeds all others. Treating the Spleen first, before addressing the specific organ failing, is a core clinical instinct.

## Why Zang-Fu Is the Basis for TCM Practice

A TCM practitioner rarely treats a symptom in isolation. A patient presenting with insomnia is not given a generic sleep herb; the practitioner maps the Zang-Fu landscape first: is it Heart Fire (vivid dreams, red tongue tip), Heart-Kidney axis failure (night sweats, anxiety), or Liver Qi stagnation (waking frustrated at 3 AM per the Organ Clock)? Each pattern points to a different organ system and demands a different herbal formula or acupoint selection.

The Zang-Fu organs are connected to the body surface via meridian channels (see [Jingmai.md](Jingmai.md)). Distress in an organ manifests along its meridian. For instance, chronic outer-thigh pain often maps to the Gallbladder meridian, signaling an underlying Liver/Gallbladder imbalance. Acupuncturists use surface points to either drain excess (heat, phlegm) or tonify a deficient organ, always guided by the Zang-Fu pattern beneath. The bridge from this synthesis page to the body surface is [Jingmai.md](Jingmai.md); the bridge to daily self-cultivation is [Qigong.md](Qigong.md).
