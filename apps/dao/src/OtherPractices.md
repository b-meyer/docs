# Daoyin, Bigu, and More

Two older body practices fill out the yangsheng - nourishing life tradition before dedicated pages on [Qigong](Qigong.md) and [Neidan](Neidan.md) take over. Daoyin is a set of guided stretches and postures documented at least by the second century BCE, and Bigu is a dietary regime that avoids grain entirely. Both aim at thinning the body's dependence on coarse matter so that [qi](Qi.md) can circulate freely and support long life.

## Daoyin - Guiding and Stretching

Daoyin literally means "guiding and pulling." The practice consists of coordinated breathing with stretching and postural movement designed to drive [qi](Qi.md) through the body's channels without obstruction. Archaeological evidence dates it firmly to the Han dynasty. Among the Mawangdui manuscripts, excavated from a tomb sealed around 168 BCE, is the Daoyin tu, a silk chart showing over forty figures in distinct postures, each labeled with the condition it treats or the animal whose posture it imitates.

Chapter 15 of the [Zhuangzi](Zhuangzi.md) gives the earliest literary reference, describing "blowing and breathing, exhaling the old and drawing in the new, the bear-hang and bird-stretch" as the concerns of those who practice yangsheng and want to live as long as Pengzu. The passage treats the practice as already established and recognizable, suggesting its roots predate the text.

Daoyin is the older substrate from which [Qigong](Qigong.md) developed. The two share the same theory of qi circulation and the same logic of coordinating breath with movement, but Qigong became the organizing term during the twentieth century and now covers a broader range of practices.

## Bigu - Avoiding Grain

Bigu means "avoiding grain." The classical five grains (rice, millet, hemp, wheat, and beans) were considered coarse and heavy, and eating them was thought to sustain the sanshi, three parasite-like entities said to inhabit the body's cinnabar fields. The sanshi, also called the three worms (san chong), were believed to report a person's faults to heaven on certain calendar days, shortening lifespan. Stopping grain intake was meant to starve them out.

In Ge Hong's Baopuzi (fourth century CE), bigu appears as one technique among many for clearing the body before more advanced cultivation. Practitioners substituted grain with pine seeds, sesame, or mineral preparations considered light and refining rather than coarsening. Some texts describe extended fasts supported only by breath, a practice that shades into the ideal described in the [Liezi](Liezi.md) of masters who subsist on qi alone.

Bigu was not primarily about abstinence. The body that no longer requires coarse food was understood to have already transformed its constitution, making it capable of the more delicate work of internal cultivation described in the [Jing, Qi, Shen](JingQiShen.md) framework.

## Yangsheng As a Category

Daoyin and Bigu both belong to the broader yangsheng category, which also includes regulated breathing (tuna - expelling old breath and drawing in new), sexual cultivation aimed at conserving jing (essence), and dietary routines tied to seasonal qi patterns. These methods were understood as preparatory, building the physical baseline required before [Meditation](Meditation.md) or [Neidan](Neidan.md) could be effective. [Celestial Masters](CelestialMasters.md) texts listed ethical conduct as a further preliminary, on the grounds that a disordered moral life creates qi stagnation no physical practice can correct.

The sanshi framework linking diet to moral accounting gave Bigu a quasi-ritual dimension absent from purely physical interpretations of health.
